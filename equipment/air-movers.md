# equipment/air-movers.md — Air Movers (Practical Field Guide)

**Purpose**: Understand how to use air movers effectively on water damage jobs. Air movement is one of the most important tools for increasing evaporation rate.

## What Air Movers Actually Do

Air movers create airflow across wet surfaces. This does two main things:

1. Moves the humid boundary layer of air away from the material.
2. Brings drier air into contact with the wet surface.

Without good airflow, even a powerful dehumidifier has limited effect because moisture can’t easily leave the material.

**My working rule**: Air movers are not just for “blowing air around the room.” They need to move air *across* the wet surfaces.

## Types of Air Movers

**Axial fans**:
- High CFM, lower static pressure.
- Good for moving large volumes of air across open floors and surfaces.
- Most common type used on water jobs.

**Centrifugal (snail) fans**:
- Lower CFM but higher static pressure.
- Better at pushing air into cavities, under cabinets, or through wall drying systems.
- Useful for more targeted airflow.

**My working rule**: Most jobs are done with axial fans. I use centrifugal fans when I need to push air into tighter spaces or wall cavities.

## Placement Principles

**Core idea**: Air should move *across* wet materials, not just circulate in the room.

**General guidelines**:
- Position fans to create cross-flow or circular patterns across wet surfaces.
- On carpet/LVP jobs, blow air under edges and across the floor.
- Angle fans upward to dry wet walls.
- Use smaller or directed fans under cabinets and toe kicks after pulling them.
- Avoid creating dead air pockets.

**My working rule**: I walk the affected area and think about where air is *not* moving. Then I place fans to fix those dead spots.

## Quantity — Rules of Thumb

There is no perfect formula, but here are practical starting points:

- **Class 2 carpet/pad jobs**: Aim for enough airflow that you feel significant air movement when standing in the middle of the room.
- **Smaller areas** (< 300–400 sq ft): 2–4 large air movers is often enough.
- **Larger open areas**: More fans, focused on creating good coverage across all wet surfaces.
- **Wall drying**: Angle fans to blow across wet wall surfaces.

**My working rule**: It’s usually better to have slightly too much airflow on day one than too little. You can always turn fans off or reposition them later.

## Interaction with Dehumidifiers

Air movers and dehumidifiers work as a team:

- Air movers move moist air off the materials and into the room.
- Dehumidifiers remove moisture from the air.
- Good airflow delivers moist air to the dehumidifier intakes and returns drier air to the materials.

**My working rule**: If the dehumidifier is running but the space isn’t drying well, the problem is often poor airflow across the wet surfaces rather than not enough dehumidification.

## Common Mistakes

- Placing fans so they just blow air in circles without hitting wet surfaces.
- Not getting airflow under LVP, carpet edges, or into cabinets.
- Using too few fans on larger or heavily wetted areas.
- Pointing fans at walls from too far away (weak airflow at the surface).
- Forgetting to reposition fans as drying progresses.

**My working rule**: I check airflow by feel. If I can’t feel strong air movement at the wet surface, the fan isn’t doing its job effectively there.

## Safety Notes

- Keep cords out of standing water.
- Use GFCI protection on all equipment.
- Secure fans so they don’t tip or get knocked over.
- Be mindful of noise and tripping hazards in occupied spaces.
