# equipment/dehumidifiers.md — Dehumidifiers (Practical Field Guide)

**Purpose**: Understand how to size, place, and use dehumidifiers effectively on water damage jobs.

Dehumidifiers remove moisture from the air. They only work well when air movers are delivering moist air to them and returning drier air to the wet materials.

## Types of Dehumidifiers

**Low Grain Refrigerant (LGR)**:
- Most common modern type used on water jobs.
- Can pull moisture down to lower grains per pound.
- Works better in cooler conditions than older conventional units.
- Generally more efficient.

**Conventional Refrigerant**:
- Older style.
- Less effective in cooler temperatures or when trying to reach very low humidity levels.

**Desiccant**:
- Uses a desiccant wheel instead of refrigeration.
- Excellent in very cool conditions or when extremely low grains are needed.
- Less common on standard residential jobs.

**My working rule**: Most jobs are handled with LGR dehumidifiers. I reach for desiccant units on specialty jobs or when working in cold conditions.

## Sizing — Rules of Thumb

There is no single perfect formula, but here are practical starting points for typical Class 2/3 residential jobs:

- **Up to ~400–500 sq ft** affected: One good LGR is often sufficient.
- **600–900 sq ft**: Two LGRs is a common starting point.
- **1000+ sq ft** or very wet/heavy saturation: Three or more, or larger capacity units.

**Adjustment factors**:
- Add more capacity if: House is very tight, starting humidity is high (>65–70% RH), or drying progress is slow after the first day.
- Can sometimes reduce later in the job once bulk moisture is removed.
- Class 4 or specialty drying often requires longer run times or supplemental heat rather than just more dehus.

**My working rule**: I tend to size slightly on the high side on day one. It’s easier to turn units off or down later than to play catch-up because I undersized.

## Placement

- Place dehumidifiers centrally to the affected area when possible.
- Make sure intake and exhaust have room to breathe (don’t block them).
- Position so dry air exhaust helps push air toward wet areas or air mover intakes.
- Run condensate drain hoses with proper slope to a safe location (sink, toilet, outside, or condensate pump).

**My working rule**: I check that the unit has good airflow around it and that the condensate drain is clear and properly routed before leaving the job.

## Monitoring Performance

**What to watch**:
- Relative Humidity (% RH) in the affected space.
- Grains Per Pound (GPP) if your meter calculates it (very useful).
- How quickly the space is drying (trend over hours/days).
- Whether moisture readings on materials are dropping.

**Signs the dehu setup isn’t working well**:
- RH or GPP isn’t dropping after several hours of proper operation.
- Space feels humid even with equipment running.
- Drying progress stalls.

**My working rule**: If the numbers aren’t moving, I look at airflow first (are air movers actually moving air across wet surfaces?), then dehu capacity, then hidden moisture sources.

## Common Mistakes

- Undersizing on day one.
- Poor placement (blocked intake/exhaust or bad airflow patterns).
- Not addressing airflow across wet surfaces (dehus can’t pull moisture they never receive).
- Ignoring condensate drain issues (clogs, kinks, improper slope).
- Leaving units running long after they’re no longer needed.

**My working rule**: I check dehumidifiers daily on active drying jobs. Just because they’re running doesn’t mean they’re still the right size or in the right place.

## Interaction with Air Movers

Dehumidifiers and air movers must work together:

- Air movers move moist air off materials.
- Dehumidifiers remove that moisture from the air.
- Good airflow delivers moist air to the dehu and returns drier air to the materials.

Without good airflow, dehumidifiers have limited impact.
