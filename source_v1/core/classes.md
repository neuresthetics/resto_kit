# core/classes.md — Classes of Water Intrusion (My Working Definitions)

**Purpose**: Class tells you how the water has interacted with the structure and materials. This drives how aggressive you need to be with extraction, drying methods, and possible material removal.

Class is **not** just "how much water." It's about absorption, wicking, and what it will take to get the materials back to dry.

## Class 1 — Minimal Impact

**Typical characteristics**:
- Small amount of water
- Mostly on low-porosity surfaces (tile, sealed concrete, vinyl with no pad)
- Very little wicking into walls, cabinets, or insulation
- Easy to extract or even just dry in place with good airflow

**Field feel**:
- You can usually see the water and where it stopped
- Minimal swelling or staining
- Quick job if addressed fast

**My approach**:
- Good extraction if any standing water
- Solid airflow + dehumidification
- Often doesn't require wall or cabinet opening
- Verify with moisture meter — don't assume because it "looks dry"

**Voice note**: "Class 1 is the easy one. Don't get lazy on verification though."

## Class 2 — Significant Absorption (Most Common)

**Typical characteristics**:
- Carpet and pad soaked
- Some wicking into drywall base, base cabinets, or furniture
- Water has spread under LVP, hardwood, or into subfloor in places
- Moderate evaporation load

**Field feel**:
- Standard residential water loss
- You need to pull carpet or at least get under it
- Walls may show moisture a few inches up
- Cabinets often need toe kicks pulled and interior checked

**My approach**:
- Aggressive extraction first (wand or portable extractor)
- Air movers positioned to blow under edges and across surfaces
- Usually 1-3 LGR dehus depending on square footage and how wet it got
- Check inside cabinets and behind baseboards — water loves to hide there
- Often salvageable without major demo if dried properly and quickly

**Voice note**: "This is bread-and-butter work. Extract hard, move air, don't underestimate how far it wicked under the LVP or into the walls."

## Class 3 — Overhead / Greatest Evaporation Load

**Typical characteristics**:
- Water came from above (ceiling leak, roof, sprinkler, upstairs overflow)
- Affects ceilings, upper walls, and often insulation in attics or ceilings
- Water has run down walls and wicked into multiple layers
- High evaporation load because ceilings and upper walls are involved

**Field feel**:
- Wet drywall on ceilings and walls
- Possible insulation saturation (especially blown-in or batts)
- Water may have run behind walls or into chases
- Often requires more invasive drying or removal

**My approach**:
- Extraction where possible (especially if there's standing water on floor from the runoff)
- Wall and ceiling drying systems (injectidry style) frequently needed
- Air movers angled up to walls and ceilings
- Higher chance of needing to remove insulation or open ceilings/walls for proper drying
- Monitor moisture in cavities — surface readings can lie

**Voice note**: "Class 3 means the water is coming from above. Plan for more equipment and possible demo. Don't just throw air movers on the floor and hope."

## Class 4 — Specialty / Dense Materials or Long-Term Saturation

**Typical characteristics**:
- Water has penetrated low-porosity or dense materials (hardwood floors, plaster, concrete, thick insulation, books, documents)
- Long-term hidden leak (weeks or months)
- Materials that don't release moisture easily with standard air mover + dehu setups
- Often requires specialty methods or partial removal

**Field feel**:
- Moisture readings stay high even after days of standard drying
- Hardwood cupping or swelling
- Concrete or plaster that feels damp deep
- Previous "drying" attempts that failed

**My approach**:
- Standard air mover + dehu often isn't enough by itself
- Longer drying times + supplemental heat to increase vapor pressure in the materials
- Specialty equipment (heat drying, injection systems, desiccants in extreme cases)
- Honest conversation with adjuster/homeowner about likely need for material replacement vs heroic drying attempts
- Verify deeply (probes, multiple locations, infrared if available)

**Voice note**: "Class 4 is where you earn your money or learn the hard way. These don't dry on normal timelines. Plan for it or you'll be back."

## Quick Field Rule (My Standard)

Class tells you the **drying strategy and equipment intensity**, not just the cleanup effort.

A small Class 3 (overhead water in one room) can be harder than a large Class 2 (big carpet job on slab).

Always combine Category + Class when deciding on PPE, containment, and drying method.
