# core/hvac-basics.md — HVAC Systems on Restoration Jobs (My Working View)

**Purpose**: Understand how HVAC systems interact with water, smoke, and mold. HVAC is one of the biggest ways contamination and moisture spread through a house, so it needs to be considered early on most jobs.

## Why HVAC Matters

HVAC systems move air — and air carries:
- Moisture
- Mold spores
- Smoke particles and odor
- Dust and debris

On many jobs, the HVAC system either helped create the problem or will make it worse if left running unchecked.

## Common HVAC Issues on Restoration Jobs

### Water Damage Jobs
- Moisture can enter the system through leaks in ducts, especially in attics or crawlspaces.
- Wet insulation around ducts or in the air handler can lead to hidden mold.
- Running the system while there is standing water or high humidity can spread moisture throughout the house.
- Condensate drains can back up and cause water damage inside or near the air handler.

**My working rule**: On water losses, I check whether the HVAC was running during or after the event. If it was, I assume moisture and possible mold may have spread through the ducts.

### Fire & Smoke Jobs
- Smoke is pulled into the return ducts and distributed through the supply vents.
- Smoke odor can linger in the ductwork and insulation around it for a long time.
- Heavy smoke can coat the inside of ducts, coils, and the air handler.
- Running the system after a fire without cleaning or sealing it will keep pushing smoke smell through the house.

**My working rule**: On most fire/smoke jobs, I recommend turning the HVAC system off until it can be assessed and cleaned (or at least until basic containment is set up).

### Mold Jobs
- Mold growing in or around the air handler, ducts, or insulation is very common on long-term water losses.
- The system can actively spread mold spores throughout the house if left running.
- Wet or dirty filters, coils, and drain pans are breeding grounds for mold and bacteria.

**My working rule**: On jobs with visible mold or musty smells, I treat the HVAC system as potentially contaminated until proven otherwise.

## Practical Field Decisions

**When to shut the system down**:
- Almost always on fire/smoke jobs until assessed.
- On water or mold jobs if there’s standing water, high humidity in the affected area, or visible mold near returns/supplies.
- When doing heavy demo or containment work.

**When to keep it running**:
- Sometimes useful for drying (with proper filtration and dehumidification).
- Can help with negative pressure setups if managed carefully.
- In very hot or cold conditions for occupant comfort (with proper controls).

**My working rule**: When in doubt, I shut it down first and figure out the best approach rather than letting it run and potentially spread the problem.

## Common HVAC Components to Check

- **Return vents & filters**: Often the dirtiest. Smoke and mold love return sides.
- **Supply vents/registers**: Can blow contaminated air directly into rooms.
- **Air handler / furnace**: Coils, drain pan, and insulation around it are common problem areas.
- **Ductwork**: Especially flex duct and duct board in attics/crawlspaces — hard to clean and prone to hidden growth.
- **Condensate drain**: Clogs are common and can cause water damage.

**My working rule**: I at least do a visual inspection of returns, supplies, and the air handler area on most water, mold, and fire jobs. If anything looks suspect, I flag it for further evaluation or specialty cleaning.

## Cleaning vs Replacement Decisions

- **Filters**: Almost always replaced.
- **Registers/vents**: Can often be removed and cleaned.
- **Ductwork**: Hard to clean properly. Heavy contamination or mold often leads to replacement of flex duct or sections of duct board.
- **Air handler / coils**: Can sometimes be cleaned by specialists, but heavily contaminated units are frequently replaced.
- **Insulation around ducts**: Wet or moldy insulation is usually removed.

**My working rule**: On anything more than light smoke or minor water exposure, I’m honest that full duct cleaning or replacement may be needed. Partial cleaning often doesn’t solve odor or mold issues.

## Working With HVAC Techs

On complex jobs, it’s often worth bringing in an HVAC technician early, especially for:
- Evaluating the air handler and coils
- Duct cleaning or sealing recommendations
- Deciding what can be saved vs replaced

**My working rule**: I don’t pretend to be an HVAC expert. On jobs where the system is heavily involved, I involve someone who is.
