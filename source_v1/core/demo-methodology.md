# core/demo-methodology.md — Demolition & Removal Methodology (My Working View)

**Purpose**: This file covers how I approach the physical removal of damaged materials during water, fire, and mold jobs. It sits between initial assessment and active drying. The goal is to remove what must go while protecting what stays, working safely, and avoiding unnecessary destruction or secondary damage.

Demo is where a lot of money is made or lost, and where a lot of mistakes happen. Done well, it sets up clean, efficient drying and restoration. Done poorly, it creates more work, more liability, and more customer issues.

## Core Demo Mindset

- **Assess before you cut**: Never start cutting or removing materials without first understanding what you’re dealing with. This includes checking for asbestos, lead, hidden utilities, and structural concerns.
- **Control the work**: Match your aggression level to the situation. Use surgical precision around sensitive areas and customer belongings. Go aggressive when doing a full gut.
- **Protect what stays**: The surfaces and materials that are not being removed are your responsibility. Protect them like they’re your own.
- **Work clean and safe**: Exposed fasteners, trip hazards, and poor staging create injuries and callbacks. Clean up as you go.
- **Think top-down and outside-in**: Follow logical sequences that reduce re-contamination and make the job easier on the people who come after you.

## Assess Before Cutting

Always verify conditions before making cuts, especially on drywall or plaster.

- Check for asbestos in drywall, popcorn ceilings, insulation, and flooring before doing flood cuts or aggressive demo on pre-1980 homes.
- You can usually safely remove baseboards and outlet covers even if asbestos is suspected, as long as the drywall itself is not being disturbed.
- When in doubt on older homes or long-term losses, assume asbestos/lead until proven otherwise or escalate to someone qualified.

**My working rule**: I do not make flood cuts or open walls aggressively until I have either confirmed the materials are safe or I am properly protected and permitted to disturb them.

## Control the Work — Surgical vs Aggressive

Not every job needs the same level of aggression.

- Use small, controlled, surgical cuts when working around electrical, plumbing, HVAC, or customer belongings.
- When doing a full gut or removing large areas of heavily damaged material, work more aggressively and remove larger pieces.
- Match your tool and technique to the material and the goal. A multi-tool with a carbide blade is often better than a reciprocating saw for precision work in lath and plaster or wood paneling.

**My working rule**: I default to controlled and precise unless the situation clearly calls for speed and volume removal. Over-cutting creates more work later.

## Remove From the Top Down

Sequence matters for safety, efficiency, and contamination control.

- Fixtures and cabinets first
- Then baseboards and trim
- Then walls (top to bottom when possible)
- Flooring last

This order reduces the chance of re-contaminating already cleaned areas and makes it easier to manage debris.

**My working rule**: I almost always work top-down unless there is a specific reason not to (structural concerns, access, or customer direction).

## Protect What Stays

Protecting finished surfaces and customer property is non-negotiable.

- Use Ram Board, heavy builder’s paper, blankets, or plywood in high-traffic areas and on surfaces that will remain.
- Secure protection with low-adhesive blue painter’s tape only — never duct tape on finished surfaces.
- Use corner guards when moving hoses, equipment, or debris that could damage walls or doorways.
- Stage dirty or contaminated materials on protection, never directly on clean floors or counters.

**My working rule**: If I wouldn’t want it done in my own house, I don’t do it on a job. Protection is cheap compared to callbacks and damaged property claims.

## Work Around Obstacles

Electrical, plumbing, and built-in items require care.

- Remove outlet covers before cutting around electrical boxes.
- Use controlled cuts and hand tools to work around boxes without damaging wiring.
- Cap open water lines with pressure caps even if the valve is off — it looks professional and reduces liability.
- When working in lath and plaster or wood paneling, expect hidden cavities, chicken wire, screws, and nails. A carbide multi-tool blade is usually the right tool.

**My working rule**: I slow down and get precise when I’m near electrical or plumbing. Rushing here creates expensive problems.

## Decision Making — Patch vs Replace

Not every damaged material needs to be fully removed.

- When deciding whether to patch or replace an entire floor (or section of drywall), check if the owner has “continuous flooring” coverage on their policy. This often determines what is economically and contractually reasonable.
- On Category 3 losses with long dwell time, be more aggressive with removal. Two or more days of sitting water is often enough time for significant bacterial and mold growth behind tile, in wall cavities, and under flooring.
- In long-dwell Category 3 jobs, cut higher than visible damage — typically 12–18 inches minimum on walls.

**My working rule**: I remove what cannot be reliably dried or cleaned within a reasonable timeframe. When in doubt on contaminated or long-dwell losses, I lean toward removal over heroic drying attempts.

## Prioritize Safety and Cleanliness

Demo creates hazards. Manage them actively.

- Remove exposed fasteners, nails, and screws before the end of the day.
- Keep work areas as organized as reasonably possible. A messy demo area is a dangerous demo area.
- Bag and remove contaminated materials promptly. Do not let debris pile up in occupied spaces.
- On Category 3 and mold jobs, double-bag or use proper containment when moving waste out of the work area.

**My working rule**: At the end of every demo day I want the site safer and cleaner than when I started. No job is worth leaving hazards behind.
