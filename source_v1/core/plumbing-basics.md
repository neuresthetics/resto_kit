# core/plumbing-basics.md — Basic Plumbing Considerations on Restoration Jobs (My Working View)

**Purpose**: Practical plumbing knowledge that comes up frequently on water damage jobs. This helps with safety, scoping, and knowing when to call a licensed plumber.

## Water Shutoffs — Know Where They Are

One of the first things you should do on most water losses is locate and verify the main water shutoff.

**Common locations**:
- Main shutoff near the water meter (often outside or in a basement/crawlspace)
- Inside the house near where the main line enters (utility room, garage, basement)
- Under kitchen or bathroom sinks (for localized shutoffs)
- At the water heater

**My working rule**: I always try to locate the main shutoff early, even if the leak appears to be isolated. It’s better to know where it is before you need it in a hurry.

**Old houses**: Shutoffs can be seized, missing handles, or located in awkward places. Don’t assume they’ll work smoothly.

## Common Plumbing Issues on Water Jobs

**Supply line failures**:
- Toilet supply lines, fridge ice maker lines, and washing machine hoses are very common failure points.
- These are often easy to replace but can cause significant damage if they fail while no one is home.

**Drain line issues**:
- Clogs, collapsed lines, or failed joints can cause slow leaks or backups.
- Older cast iron or galvanized drains are prone to corrosion and leaks.

**Appliance-related**:
- Washing machines, dishwashers, water heaters, and ice makers are frequent sources of water loss.
- Hose failures and drain connection issues are common.

**Fixture failures**:
- Toilet tank components, faucet cartridges, and shower valves can fail over time.

**My working rule**: When investigating a water loss, I look at the most common failure points first (supply lines, appliance connections, and obvious wet areas) before assuming a more complex issue.

## When to Involve a Licensed Plumber

You don’t need a plumber for every water loss, but there are times when you should bring one in:

- The source of the leak is not obvious or accessible.
- There is significant damage to plumbing lines (not just a supply hose).
- The job involves modifying or rerouting plumbing.
- There are signs of major issues (low pressure, multiple leaks, sewage involvement).
- The homeowner or adjuster wants a professional plumbing assessment.

**My working rule**: If I’m unsure about the condition of the plumbing or if the repair goes beyond replacing a supply line or fixture, I recommend involving a licensed plumber early.

## Old Plumbing Considerations

**Galvanized steel pipe**:
- Common in older homes. Prone to corrosion and restricted flow over time.
- Can hide leaks inside walls or under floors.

**Polybutylene (PB) pipe**:
- Used in some homes from the late 1970s to mid-1990s. Known for failures at fittings.
- If you see it, flag it — it may affect scope and long-term reliability.

**Copper pipe**:
- Generally reliable but can develop pinhole leaks, especially in certain water conditions.

**PEX tubing**:
- Common in newer construction. Generally reliable but can be damaged by improper installation or physical impact.

**My working rule**: On older homes, I note the type of plumbing and any visible signs of age or deterioration. This helps set expectations with the homeowner and adjuster.

## Practical Field Habits

- Always try to stop the water source as quickly and safely as possible.
- Use proper tools and techniques when working around plumbing (avoid damaging lines while doing demo).
- Be careful not to create new leaks while moving or working around fixtures and appliances.
- Document the condition of plumbing components you encounter, especially if they look old or damaged.

**My working rule**: I treat plumbing systems with respect. It’s easy to make a small problem worse while trying to fix the water damage.

## Backflow & Cross-Connection Awareness

On some jobs (especially Category 3 or commercial), there can be backflow or cross-connection concerns.

- Avoid creating situations where contaminated water can flow back into the clean water supply.
- Be cautious with garden hoses, pressure washers, and equipment that could create backflow.

**My working rule**: On higher contamination jobs, I’m extra careful about how water is being moved and where it’s going.
