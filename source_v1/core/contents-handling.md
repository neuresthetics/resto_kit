# core/contents-handling.md — Contents Assessment & Handling (My Working View)

**Purpose**: Practical guidance on how to deal with personal property (contents) on water, fire, and smoke jobs. Covers assessment, pack-out decisions, cleaning vs replacement, documentation, and common field pitfalls.

Contents work is often more emotionally charged than structural work. Homeowners care deeply about their stuff. How you handle it directly affects trust, callbacks, and complaints.

## Pack-Out vs Clean-in-Place

**Clean-in-place** (preferred when possible):
- Item is not heavily damaged
- Can be cleaned effectively on site without risk of further damage or cross-contamination
- Lower cost and less disruption for the homeowner
- Common for: hard surfaces, some upholstered items with light smoke, non-porous goods

**Pack-out** (necessary in many cases):
- Heavy smoke, water, or mold contamination
- Item needs specialty cleaning, ozone/hydroxyl treatment, or controlled drying
- Risk of cross-contamination if left in the structure
- Common for: clothing, documents, electronics, porous furniture, artwork, rugs

**My default bias**: When in doubt on fire/smoke jobs, pack out more rather than less. Smoke penetrates deeper than most people realize. It's easier to explain a thorough pack-out than to deal with items that still smell or have hidden damage later.

## Initial Contents Assessment

Do this early, ideally with the homeowner present:

1. **Quick visual scan** of the affected areas
2. **Ask the homeowner** what matters most to them (sentimental items, important documents, electronics, etc.)
3. **Photograph everything** before touching — wide shots and close-ups of damage
4. **Note pre-existing damage** (scratches, stains, wear) so it doesn't get blamed on you later
5. **Identify high-value or irreplaceable items** immediately (jewelry, important papers, family photos, medications)

**Voice note**: "On fire jobs especially, take photos of everything before you move it. The smoke damage pattern can be important later, and homeowners sometimes forget what was already damaged."

## Documentation & Inventory

Good documentation protects everyone:

- Detailed photo inventory (before, during, after)
- Written list with descriptions and condition notes
- Clear labeling of boxes/bins with contents and room of origin
- Chain of custody if items are going to a cleaning facility or storage
- Separate "pre-existing damage" notes

**Common mistake**: Rushing the pack-out without good photos. This leads to disputes about what was damaged and when.

## Common Decision Factors

**Clean**:
- Light to moderate smoke residue on cleanable surfaces
- Water damage that can be dried or cleaned without structural compromise
- Non-porous or semi-porous items in good condition

**Replace**:
- Heavy charring or structural damage to the item
- Significant mold growth on porous materials
- Electronics with corrosion risk (especially after acidic smoke + moisture)
- Items with strong embedded odor that won't come out
- Heavily contaminated Category 3 water items (sewage, serious biohazards)

**Special handling**:
- **Documents & photos**: Freeze drying or specialty document restoration is often worth it for important papers. Normal drying can cause inks to run or pages to stick.
- **Electronics**: High failure rate after smoke exposure. Many get replaced even if they power on initially due to hidden corrosion.
- **Clothing & fabrics**: Can often be cleaned, but heavy smoke or protein smoke can be very difficult. Test small areas when possible.
- **Wood furniture**: Can sometimes be cleaned and refinished, but swelling, cracking, or deep smoke penetration often makes replacement more practical.
- **Mattresses & upholstered furniture**: Usually replaced on anything more than very light smoke or water exposure.

## Security & Chain of Custody

When doing pack-outs:

- Use numbered bins/boxes with clear labeling
- Keep a master inventory list
- Secure storage (locked truck, warehouse, or on-site secure area)
- Get homeowner sign-off on the inventory when possible
- Document who handled what and when

**Field reality**: Theft claims happen. Good documentation and secure handling dramatically reduce problems.

## My Working Rules on Contents

1. **Photograph everything before you touch it.** Wide shots + detail shots. This is non-negotiable on anything valuable or disputed.

2. **Talk to the homeowner early** about what matters most to them. People will accept losing some things if their most important items are handled with care.

3. **Over-document rather than under-document.** Good photos and lists save more headaches than they create.

4. **When in doubt on fire/smoke, pack out.** Smoke damage is sneaky. It's better to clean off-site in controlled conditions than to leave items and hope.

5. **Be honest about cleanability.** Don't promise results you can't deliver on heavily contaminated porous items. It leads to angry callbacks.

6. **Electronics and documents are special.** These often need specialty vendors. Know when to call them in early.

## Voice Notes & Prompts

**Voice note**: "Contents work is where you either build trust or destroy it. Homeowners remember how you handled their stuff more than how straight you hung the drywall."

**Voice usage examples**:
- "Heavy smoke throughout the house from a kitchen fire — pack-out strategy for contents?"
- "Water damage to bedroom, some smoke residue from previous fire — clean in place or pack out the clothing and bedding?"
- "Homeowner has important family photos and documents affected — what's the realistic path?"
