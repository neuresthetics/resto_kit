# core/initial-assessment-principles.md — Initial Assessment Principles (My Working View)

**Purpose**: This file covers the principles and key decisions I make during the first on-site evaluation of a water loss. Initial assessment is the most important phase of the job. Everything that follows — containment, extraction, demo, and drying — is shaped by what I get right (or wrong) in the first 30–90 minutes.

The goal is to quickly understand the loss, identify immediate hazards, classify the water, determine realistic scope for Day 1, and establish the right level of engineering controls before any intrusive work begins.

## Core Assessment Mindset

- **Signed authorization first, always**: No physical work of any kind begins until the responsible party has signed the work authorization and any required releases. This includes shutting off water or setting up equipment.
- **Assume hidden problems**: Cause of loss is frequently hidden. Water often comes from internal sources rather than obvious external ones. Use systematic investigation rather than assumptions.
- **Err toward caution on category and contamination**: When in doubt, treat the loss as higher risk until proven otherwise. It is easier to reduce protection than to increase it later.
- **Day 1 is not about finishing the job**: On most emergency water losses, Day 1 is limited to paperwork, source control, bulk extraction, basic containment, and drying equipment setup. Full demo and detailed mapping usually happen on Day 2.
- **Protect the path**: Floor protection and containment setup should happen early to prevent tracking contamination through unaffected areas.

## Signed Authorization Before Any Physical Work

The material interested party (homeowner or authorized representative) must sign the work authorization before the technician touches anything.

This includes:
- Shutting off water at the angle stop or main valve
- Setting up any extraction or drying equipment
- Moving or disturbing contents
- Making any cuts or removals

**My working rule**: I do not begin physical work until the authorization is signed. No exceptions. This protects everyone and sets clear expectations from the start.

## Arrival, Staging & Initial Safety

Before entering or setting up:

- Perform a 360 of the exterior for obvious hazards (downed power lines, structural damage, blocked access, standing water near electrical).
- Identify a safe staging area, preferably upwind and uphill from the affected zone when possible.
- Don minimum PPE (gloves, safety glasses, steel-toe boots, high-visibility vest) before entering.
- Note any immediate life-safety or structural concerns that would prevent safe entry.

If immediate hazards exist, secure the perimeter and do not proceed until cleared by the appropriate party.

## Customer Interview & Loss History

Talk to the customer or responsible party early and document key information:

- When did the water event start and when was it discovered?
- What was the claimed source?
- How long has the water been sitting?
- Any previous water issues or known mold in the structure?
- Are there any occupant health symptoms (respiratory, skin, etc.)?
- Any known pre-existing conditions?

This information helps determine water category, likely hidden damage, and whether the loss is fast or slow.

**My working rule**: I treat the customer interview as part of the investigation, not just paperwork. The answers often reveal whether this is a simple clean water loss or something more complex.

## Source Isolation & Verification

After shutting off the water supply, verify that the water has actually stopped.

- For toilet supply line failures: Open the tank and hold the flapper open to confirm no additional water is entering.
- For other sources: Visually confirm at the point of failure when possible.
- Do not assume the valve or angle stop fully stopped the flow without verification.

**My working rule**: Visual confirmation at the source is required before moving into extraction or detailed assessment. Moving too fast here can make the problem worse.

## Hazard Identification & Water Category

Determine the water category as early as possible using source, appearance, odor, and time factors:

- **Category 1 (Clean)**: Sanitary sources, clear appearance, no odor. Lower containment usually sufficient.
- **Category 2 (Gray)**: Some contamination (appliance discharge, etc.). Elevated PPE and possible containment.
- **Category 3 (Black)**: Grossly contaminated or sewage. High risk. Requires containment and negative air before intrusive work.

Long dwell time (especially >48–72 hours in warm conditions) increases risk even on originally cleaner water due to bacterial and mold growth.

On Category 2 or 3 losses, apply disinfectant spray and perform surface wiping of affected areas before conducting the full moisture survey or moving extensively through the space.

**My working rule**: I classify conservatively. If there is any doubt between Category 2 and 3, or if the loss has been sitting for multiple days, I default to higher protection and containment until I have more information.

## Realistic Day 1 Scope

On most emergency water losses, Day 1 is limited to:

- Signed paperwork and customer communication
- Source isolation
- Bulk water extraction
- Basic containment where it meaningfully reduces air volume
- Drying equipment setup (air movers + dehumidifiers)

Full demo, detailed moisture mapping, and aggressive wall/ceiling removal usually happen on Day 2 after equipment has run for 12–24 hours.

Trying to do everything in one visit is often unrealistic and can lead to poor decisions or cross-contamination.

**My working rule**: I set realistic expectations with the customer and myself on Day 1. The priority is stopping the spread of water and starting the drying process safely.

## Early Stabilization & Physical Protection

Address immediate safety and contamination risks early:

- If a ceiling is visibly sagging from water weight, set up a zip pole or equivalent support as soon as possible — ideally before or immediately after shutting off the water.
- Before bringing extraction equipment, dehumidifiers, or tools through finished hallways or unaffected areas, lay down proper floor protection (Ram Board, plastic, etc.) and corner guards.
- This prevents tracking contamination and damaging finished surfaces during the critical first day.

**My working rule**: I stabilize obvious hazards (sagging ceilings) and protect clean paths before doing heavy work. Prevention is faster than repair.

## Initial Moisture Readings & Detection

On first arrival, moisture meter readings are taken on whatever material is currently exposed (carpet, vinyl, tile, etc.).

You cannot reliably read the subfloor until the surface material is removed or lifted. Document readings accordingly and note the material being measured.

Use pinless mode on tile and hard surfaces. Compare readings to known dry areas. Be cautious with fiber cement board and grout lines, as they can give false readings.

**My working rule**: Initial readings are surface readings. I document what I measured and on what material. Hidden moisture investigation comes after extraction and basic controls are in place.

## Voice Usage Examples

- "Customer just signed authorization — what are the first things I should verify before touching anything?"
- "Toilet supply line failure. How do I confirm the water is actually off before I start extraction?"
- "Water has been sitting for three days with a musty smell. How should I approach category and containment on this one?"
- "Sagging ceiling in the living room — should I stabilize that before or after shutting off the water?"
- "What realistically needs to happen on Day 1 versus what can wait until tomorrow?"
- "Older home, possible asbestos — how does that change my initial assessment approach?"
