# core/dry-standards.md — What "Dry" Actually Means (My Standards)

**Purpose**: Clear target for when a job is done drying. Prevents both under-drying (callbacks, mold) and over-drying / unnecessary work.

## Core Principle

"Dry" is not "bone dry" or "factory new." It means the affected materials have been brought back to a moisture level consistent with **unaffected materials in the same environment**, or to the accepted equilibrium moisture content (EMC) for that material and the current conditions.

You are returning the structure to a stable, normal state for that building and climate — not turning it into a desert.

## Practical Targets (My Field Standards)

**General residential goal**:
- Bring moisture readings in affected areas to within a few percentage points of unaffected areas in the same room or adjacent unaffected space.
- For wood and wood products: Usually back to the 8-12% range depending on region and season (lower in dry climates, higher in humid ones). Check unaffected wood in the same house as your reference.
- For drywall, plaster, concrete, etc.: Return to "normal" for that material in that building. Concrete slabs can read higher naturally — know your baseline.

**Verification methods** (use more than one when possible):
- Pin or pinless moisture meter on multiple surfaces and depths
- Thermo-hygrometer readings (RH, temp, GPP) in the space over time
- Visual and touch inspection (no musty smell, no soft spots, no visible moisture)
- Infrared camera (great for spotting temperature differences that indicate hidden moisture)
- For critical jobs: Data logging over several days to show trend toward equilibrium

## Red Flags That It's Not Actually Dry

- Moisture readings still climbing or holding steady after equipment has been running properly for days
- Visible mold growth appearing during or after "drying"
- Musty odor that wasn't there before or is getting worse
- Warping, cupping, or swelling that continues
- Homeowner reports new issues (allergy symptoms, smell) after you left

If any of these appear, you either missed hidden moisture or the drying approach wasn't sufficient for the materials involved.

## My Working Rules

1. **Document your baseline**. Take readings in unaffected areas early. This is your target, not some universal number from a chart.
2. **Don't declare dry on day 1 or 2 just because equipment is running**. Some jobs (especially Class 3 and 4) take time. Let the trend tell you.
3. **Over-drying can cause problems too** (wood shrinkage, cracking, unnecessary demo). Once materials are back in the normal range for the environment and stable, stop.
4. **Hidden cavities lie**. Surface readings on drywall can look good while insulation or the back side is still wet. Probe or use other methods when Class 3 or when readings don't make sense.
5. **Communicate the standard**. When talking to homeowners or adjusters, say: "We're bringing the affected materials back to the same moisture level as the unaffected parts of the house." That's a clear, defensible target.

## When to Call It

A job is dry enough to remove equipment when:
- Moisture readings have stabilized in the target range
- Trend over 24+ hours shows no rebound
- No visible or olfactory signs of ongoing moisture issues
- You've addressed any hidden areas you opened or suspected

Then document it and get the equipment out. Leaving it running "just in case" wastes resources and can sometimes mask problems.
