# core/mold-conditions.md — Mold Growth Conditions on Water Damage Jobs (My Working View)

**Purpose**: Understand when and why mold becomes a real factor on water jobs. This drives decisions on containment, PPE, material removal vs drying, and when to escalate. Written for field use in Tech and Deep modes.

Mold is secondary damage. It happens when the conditions are right and time passes. The goal is to recognize the conditions early so you don’t turn a water job into a much bigger (and more expensive) mold job.

## Core Conditions Mold Needs

Mold needs **four things** to grow. Remove or limit any one of them and growth slows or stops.

1. **Moisture** (the biggest driver)
   - Most molds need sustained moisture in materials (not just humid air).
   - Porous materials (drywall, insulation, carpet pad, wood) hold moisture and become food + home for mold.
   - Visible water is obvious. Hidden moisture behind walls, under floors, or in cavities is where most problems hide.

2. **Temperature**
   - Most common indoor molds grow well between roughly 68–86°F (20–30°C).
   - Warmer conditions speed up growth. Cooler conditions slow it down but don’t stop it entirely.
   - In hot attics or poorly conditioned spaces, mold can explode fast once moisture is present.

3. **Time**
   - Rule of thumb most techs use: Significant mold risk starts after **24–48 hours** of sustained moisture in warm conditions.
   - Some molds can begin growing in as little as 24 hours on very wet, nutrient-rich materials.
   - After 72+ hours, especially in warm/humid environments, you should assume mold is or will be present even if you can’t see it yet.

4. **Nutrients / Substrate**
   - Mold eats organic material: paper facing on drywall, wood, carpet, dust, cellulose insulation, etc.
   - Non-porous surfaces (tile, sealed concrete, metal, glass) are much less likely to support growth unless they have a layer of dirt or organic film on them.

## How Water Jobs Turn Into Mold Concerns

**Category 1 (Clean)**: Can still grow mold if it sits long enough. Clean water + time + warmth = mold food.

**Category 2 (Gray)**: Higher risk because it already has some organic content and nutrients. The line between Cat 2 and “mold job” blurs quickly after 48 hours.

**Category 3 (Black)**: Often already has mold or conditions that will produce it fast. These jobs usually require more aggressive material removal rather than drying attempts.

**Key field reality**: A fresh water loss can become a mold concern in 2–3 days if conditions are right. A job that “sat over the weekend” is rarely just a water job anymore.

## Red Flags for Hidden or Active Mold

- Musty or earthy smell (even if you can’t see growth)
- Visible mold colonies (black, green, white, orange — color doesn’t tell you the species reliably)
- Water has been present >48–72 hours in warm conditions
- Previous water damage that was “dried” but still smells or has staining
- Occupant reports new allergy symptoms, sinus issues, or musty smell since the water event
- Moisture readings stay high in cavities even after equipment has been running
- Water came from a source with organic content (sewage, flood with debris, long-standing gray water)

**Hidden mold warning**: Just because you don’t see it on the surface doesn’t mean it isn’t growing behind drywall, under flooring, or in insulation. Surface readings can look fine while the back side of the drywall is covered.

## My Working Rules on Mold Conditions

1. **Time is the enemy.** Assume mold risk increases every day the materials stay wet, especially above 70°F and with poor airflow.

2. **When in doubt on time or hidden moisture, treat it as higher risk.** Better to contain and remove suspect materials than to dry and hope.

3. **Category + Time + Warmth = Mold probability.** Use this simple mental equation on every job.

4. **Document conditions and timeline early.** Photos of moisture, smell notes, and when the water started help later if mold becomes an issue.

5. **Don’t try to dry moldy materials in place** in most cases. Source removal (cutting out affected material) is usually the cleaner, faster, and more defensible approach.

6. **Ventilation and airflow matter.** Stagnant, warm, humid air is mold’s best friend. Moving air and controlling humidity slows growth even before you fully dry the materials.

## Voice Notes & Prompts

**Voice note**: "If it’s been wet more than two days in a warm house and I can’t see the back sides of the materials, I’m assuming mold is either already there or coming fast."

**Voice usage examples**:
- "Water loss from last Thursday, now Monday, finished basement, some musty smell — what’s my mold risk and approach?"
- "Category 2, 60 hours old, 78°F inside, carpet and pad still wet — conditions for mold?"
- "Hidden moisture behind wet drywall for three days — do I open it or try to dry?"
