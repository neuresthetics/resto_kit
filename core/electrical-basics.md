# core/electrical-basics.md — Basic Electrical Considerations on Restoration Jobs (My Working View)

**Purpose**: Practical electrical knowledge that comes up on water and fire jobs. This supports safety decisions and helps you know when to involve a licensed electrician.

## GFCI Protection — Non-Negotiable

On any job involving water (or even potential water), all equipment must be protected by GFCI.

- Use GFCI pigtails or plug equipment into GFCI-protected outlets.
- Never bypass or ignore GFCI requirements.
- This applies even on “clean” water jobs.

**My working rule**: If it plugs in and there’s water involved, it needs GFCI protection. No exceptions.

## Common Electrical Hazards on These Jobs

**Water + Electricity**:
- Standing water near outlets, switches, light fixtures, or appliances is extremely dangerous.
- Water can travel through walls and reach electrical components you can’t see.

**Fire-Damaged Electrical**:
- Burned wiring, panels, and outlets are common after fires.
- Damaged electrical systems can remain energized and hazardous.
- Aluminum wiring (common in some homes from the 1960s–1970s) requires extra caution.

**Hidden Issues**:
- Water can enter electrical boxes, junction boxes, and panels from above or behind.
- Corrosion on connections can develop over time after water exposure.

**My working rule**: I treat any electrical component that has been exposed to water or fire as potentially compromised until verified safe by someone qualified.

## When to Involve a Licensed Electrician

You should bring in an electrician when:

- There is standing water near electrical panels, outlets, or switches.
- Electrical components have been burned or heavily smoked.
- You need to verify power is off to an area.
- There is damaged or exposed wiring.
- The job requires moving, modifying, or repairing electrical components.
- Aluminum wiring is present and work is needed in those areas.

**My working rule**: If I’m unsure about the safety of the electrical system or if the work goes beyond basic equipment setup, I involve a licensed electrician early.

## Practical Field Habits

- Verify power status before working in wet or fire-damaged areas.
- Use a non-contact voltage tester when appropriate.
- Keep extension cords and equipment out of standing water.
- Don’t assume that because something “looks fine” it is safe after water or fire exposure.
- Document any electrical concerns you observe.

**My working rule**: I slow down around electrical systems. Rushing electrical decisions is how people get hurt.

## Old Electrical Systems

**Aluminum wiring**:
- Used in many homes built between ~1965–1975.
- Requires special connectors and handling. Can be a fire hazard if not addressed properly.
- If you see it, note it and be cautious about disturbing connections.

**Knob-and-tube wiring**:
- Found in very old homes. Fragile and often not up to modern code.
- Water exposure makes it even more hazardous.

**My working rule**: On older homes, I note the type of electrical system and flag anything that looks outdated or damaged.

## Lockout / Tagout Awareness

On some jobs (especially commercial or when working on electrical systems), proper lockout/tagout procedures should be followed.

Even on residential jobs, clearly communicating that power is off to an area helps prevent accidents.

**My working rule**: If power needs to be shut off for safety, I make sure it’s done properly and that everyone on site knows.
