# core/documentation-standards.md — Documentation Standards for Restoration Jobs (My Working View)

**Purpose**: Clear standards for what and how to document on every job. Good documentation protects you, supports the claim, and prevents disputes later.

Poor documentation is one of the most common reasons techs get into trouble with homeowners, adjusters, or their own company.

## Why Documentation Matters

- It creates a record of the condition of the property before, during, and after your work.
- It protects against false claims of damage.
- It supports accurate scoping and pricing.
- It helps when there are disputes about pre-existing conditions or scope changes.
- Good photos and notes make your job easier if you have to go back or explain what happened.

**My working rule**: If it’s important, document it. If you’re unsure whether to document something, document it anyway.

## What to Photograph

**Before starting work (most important)**:
- Overall condition of affected areas
- Any pre-existing damage (stains, wear, previous repairs, etc.)
- Water source or origin of the loss (when visible)
- Moisture meter readings with context photos
- HVAC returns, supplies, and air handler area
- Any areas that might be disputed later

**During work**:
- Demo in progress (especially hidden damage that’s revealed)
- Conditions that affect scope (more damage than expected, rot, mold, etc.)
- Work being performed (containment setup, equipment placement, etc.)

**After work / at completion**:
- Final condition of affected areas
- Any areas left for the homeowner or other trades to address
- Equipment left on site

**My working rule**: I take more photos than I think I need. It’s easier to delete extra photos later than to wish I had taken them.

## Photo Best Practices

- Take both wide/context shots and close-up/detail shots.
- Include something in the photo for scale when helpful (tape measure, glove, phone, etc.).
- Label or organize photos by room/area when possible.
- Take photos in good lighting when you can.
- If something looks questionable, photograph it from multiple angles.

**My working rule**: I try to make photos clear enough that someone who wasn’t on the job can understand what they’re looking at.

## What to Document in Writing / Notes

- Date and time of key events (arrival, discovery of hidden damage, conversations with homeowner, etc.)
- Homeowner statements about pre-existing conditions or prior events
- Moisture readings with locations and times
- Any scope changes or additional work discovered
- Conversations with adjusters, property managers, or other trades
- Equipment used and settings (especially on drying jobs)
- Any safety concerns or incidents

**My working rule**: If I have a conversation that affects scope, timeline, or expectations, I write it down with the date and time.

## Common Things Techs Forget to Document

- Pre-existing damage
- Homeowner statements (“that stain has been there for years”)
- Moisture readings in unaffected areas (baseline)
- Condition of HVAC system
- Areas that were inaccessible or not addressed
- Conversations about what the homeowner is responsible for
- Equipment left on site and expected removal date

**My working rule**: I try to document the things that could be argued about later — pre-existing damage, scope disagreements, and homeowner requests.

## Handling Pre-Existing Conditions in Documentation

- Clearly separate new damage from old damage in your photos and notes.
- Photograph pre-existing damage thoroughly before starting work.
- Note any statements the homeowner makes about prior damage.
- Don’t include pre-existing items in your scope without clear communication and documentation.

**My working rule**: I want my documentation to clearly show what I am and am not responsible for.

## Communication & Documentation

- When you point out pre-existing damage or scope limitations to the homeowner, document that conversation.
- If an adjuster or property manager gives direction, note who said what and when.
- Written follow-up (text or email) after important verbal conversations can be very useful.

**My working rule**: I try to have important conversations in writing when possible, or at least document them immediately after.

## Tools & Habits

- Use your phone’s camera — it’s usually good enough.
- Consider a small notebook or a notes app for quick field notes.
- Organize photos by job or by room when you can.
- Review your photos and notes at the end of the day while everything is fresh.

**My working rule**: Documentation is part of the job, not something extra. The better I document, the fewer problems I have later.

**Voice usage examples**:
- "Homeowner is claiming some old damage is from this loss — how should I document this?"
- "I found more damage than expected behind the cabinets — what do I need to document?"
- "Adjuster told me to do something different than the original scope — how do I document this?"

Good documentation habits will save you more headaches than almost anything else on this job. Update this file with situations you run into in the field.
