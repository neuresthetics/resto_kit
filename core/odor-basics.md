# core/odor-basics.md — Odor Basics on Restoration Jobs (My Working View)

**Purpose**: Understand how odors work on fire, smoke, and water/mold jobs. Odor is one of the most common complaints and one of the hardest things to fully resolve.

This file covers the basics of why odors persist and how to approach them in the field.

## Why Odors Are Difficult

Odors are caused by volatile organic compounds (VOCs) that are released into the air. On restoration jobs, these compounds can:

- Penetrate deeply into porous materials (drywall, insulation, wood, carpet, fabrics, ductwork)
- Get trapped behind walls, under floors, and in voids
- Bond to surfaces and continue releasing smell over time
- Be re-activated by moisture or heat

**My working rule**: If you can smell it, the source is still releasing odor compounds. Masking or covering it up rarely solves the problem long-term.

## Common Sources of Odor on Jobs

**Fire & Smoke Jobs**:
- Smoke residue (especially protein smoke and wet smoke)
- Burned materials (plastics, treated wood, insulation, etc.)
- Smoke that has penetrated HVAC systems and ductwork
- Charred structural materials

**Water & Mold Jobs**:
- Mold and bacterial growth (musty, earthy smell)
- Sewage or organic contamination (Category 3)
- Long-term moisture in materials (even without visible mold)
- Dirty or wet insulation, carpet pad, or drywall

**Combined Issues**:
- Fire + water from firefighters = very difficult odor problems (smoke + moisture + potential mold)

**My working rule**: On fire jobs that also had significant water from suppression, I expect odor issues to be worse and plan accordingly.

## Factors That Affect Odor

- **Porosity of materials**: More porous = more odor retention (insulation, drywall, fabrics, carpet vs tile, glass, metal).
- **Time**: Longer exposure = deeper penetration.
- **Moisture**: Moisture can reactivate or worsen odors.
- **Temperature**: Heat can increase the release of odor compounds.
- **Airflow**: Poor airflow lets odors linger and concentrate.

**My working rule**: I consider the materials involved and how long they were exposed when deciding how aggressive to be with odor control.

## Basic Approaches to Odor

**Source removal**:
- Removing the materials that are holding the odor (best long-term solution when feasible).
- Common on heavy smoke or mold jobs (cut out drywall, remove insulation, replace carpet/pad, etc.).

**Cleaning**:
- Cleaning surfaces to remove odor-causing residue.
- Works better on non-porous or semi-porous surfaces.

**Sealing / encapsulation**:
- Applying sealers to trap odor compounds in materials that can’t be removed.
- Used on structural wood, subfloors, or concrete in some cases.

**Air treatment**:
- Air scrubbers, ozone generators, hydroxyl generators.
- These can help reduce airborne odor compounds but usually don’t eliminate the source.

**My working rule**: I prefer source removal when possible. Air treatment and sealing are tools, not complete solutions by themselves.

## Realistic Expectations

- Some odors can be fully eliminated.
- Some odors can be significantly reduced but not completely removed.
- Some odors (especially heavy protein smoke or long-term mold in porous materials) may never fully go away without major removal.

**My working rule**: I try to be honest with homeowners and adjusters about what is realistically achievable. Over-promising on odor removal leads to callbacks and unhappy customers.

## Common Odor Mistakes

- Relying only on air treatment or “odor bombs” without addressing the source.
- Not checking HVAC systems (a very common place for odor to hide and spread).
- Sealing over odor without proper cleaning first.
- Ignoring insulation or materials behind walls/ceilings.

**My working rule**: If the odor is strong, I look for the source in hidden areas (ducts, insulation, behind walls, under floors) before relying on air treatment alone.
