# Containment Building

## Purpose
Containment is the physical barrier system used to isolate the affected area, prevent cross-contamination, and protect unaffected parts of the structure. Proper containment controls the spread of Category 2/3 water, mold spores, odors, and other contaminants during extraction, demo, drying, and remediation.

Containment is a **control measure**, not a substitute for proper extraction, cleaning, and drying.

## When Containment is Required
- Category 2 or Category 3 water losses
- Mold remediation (active growth or suspected)
- Any job where contaminants could spread to unaffected areas
- When required by IICRC S-500, company policy, or insurance scope
- Jobs involving significant demo or high airborne particulate

## When Containment May Not Be Needed
- Category 1 clean water with limited migration and no risk of cross-contamination
- Very small, contained losses where affected area is already isolated by layout
- Some contents-only jobs with no structural impact

## Types of Containment
- **Full Containment** — Complete enclosure of the affected area with floor-to-ceiling barriers, airlocks, and negative air if required.
- **Limited / Spot Containment** — Targeted barriers around specific affected zones (e.g., one room or section of a larger space).
- **Floor Protection Only** — Plastic sheeting and tape on floors and corners when full containment is not yet practical.
- **Crawlspace / Attic Containment** — Specialized setups for below-grade or overhead spaces.

## Materials
- 6-mil or thicker fire-retardant poly sheeting (clear or black)
- Poly tape (duct tape is acceptable for short-term; better tapes exist for long jobs)
- ZipWall poles or equivalent spring-loaded supports
- Floor protection film or ram board
- Corner guards / edge protection
- Negative air machine (if required) with HEPA filtration
- Air scrubber / air filtration device
- Warning signs and re-entry tags
- GFCI protection for all electrical equipment inside containment

## Recommended Sequence (Real-World Adjusted)
Most experienced techs follow this practical order:

1. **Immediate Protection (upon arrival)**  
   Lay floor protection and corner tape/covers first. This prevents tracking contaminants and protects surfaces before any work begins.

2. **Extraction First (before full containment)**  
   Primary goal is to stop the spread of water as fast as possible. Setting up full containment (plastic, poles, tape) is difficult and messy once surfaces are already wet. Most techs extract first, then build containment to isolate the COL air volume.

3. **Build Containment After Extraction**  
   Once bulk water is removed and the area is safe to work in, erect the actual barrier walls, seal penetrations, and establish negative pressure if required.

4. **HVAC Isolation**  
   Turn off and seal supply and return vents in the affected area. Tape off registers and returns. Consider isolating the entire HVAC zone if cross-contamination risk is high.

## Step-by-Step Building Process
1. Clear the work area of loose debris and trip hazards.
2. Protect floors with poly or ram board, taping all seams and edges.
3. Install vertical barriers using ZipWall poles or equivalent. Extend poly from floor to ceiling.
4. Seal all seams, edges, and penetrations with poly tape. Pay special attention to:
   - Door frames and thresholds
   - Electrical outlets and switches
   - HVAC registers and returns
   - Windows and other openings
5. Create an airlock / decon chamber at the entry point if full containment is required.
6. Set up negative air machine (if required) and verify airflow direction with smoke or flutter strip.
7. Post warning signs and document setup with photos.
8. Perform a final integrity check before starting work inside.

## Safety Requirements
- Full PPE appropriate for the Category/Class (respirator, gloves, eye protection, Tyvek or equivalent).
- GFCI protection on all electrical equipment inside containment.
- Proper ventilation and monitoring of negative pressure.
- Clear emergency egress routes maintained at all times.
- Stop-work authority if containment integrity is compromised or hazards are discovered.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Building full containment before extraction on wet jobs (makes setup harder and slower).
- Poor sealing — gaps around doors, outlets, or HVAC allow contaminant migration.
- Using containment as a substitute for actual cleaning and drying.
- Forgetting to protect or isolate HVAC systems.
- Over-building containment on small jobs where limited protection would suffice.
- Not accounting for the extra time and materials containment adds to the job.

## Real-World Observations
- Extraction before containment is standard field practice on most water jobs because stopping water migration takes priority and wet plastic is difficult to work with.
- Many techs use a two-stage approach: quick floor protection + extraction first, then proper containment after the area is drier.
- Over-containment slows jobs down and increases cost. Match the containment level to the actual risk.
- Negative air is often over-used on small jobs. Use it when airborne contaminants are a real concern, not by default.
