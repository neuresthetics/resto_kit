# core/fire-damage.md — Fire & Smoke Damage Basics (My Working View)

**Purpose**: Practical field orientation for fire and smoke losses. Covers safety, damage assessment, smoke types, and common decision points. Written in operator language for voice use.

Fire jobs are different from water jobs. The damage is often more complex because of smoke penetration, corrosion, odor, and structural compromise. Water from firefighters is also extremely common and creates secondary water + smoke problems.

## Core Safety Priorities on Fire Jobs (Non-Negotiable)

Fire jobs have more immediate structural and hazardous material risks than most water jobs.

**Structural integrity**:
- Burned or charred floor joists, roof structures, or load-bearing walls
- Sagging or spongy floors where fire burned underneath
- Compromised stairs or entry points
- Always do a careful visual and physical assessment before loading heavy equipment or walking deep into the structure

**Electrical & utility hazards**:
- Burned wiring, panels, or outlets
- Power still on in damaged areas (very common)
- Gas lines or propane near fire origin

**Hazardous materials**:
- Asbestos in older homes (popcorn ceilings, insulation, flooring, ductwork)
- Lead paint in pre-1978 homes
- PCBs in old electrical equipment or fluorescent lights
- Unknown chemicals in garages, workshops, or storage areas

**My rule**: On any fire job, especially in older homes, assume asbestos/lead until proven otherwise if you're going to disturb materials. Stop and escalate if you're not set up for it.

## Types of Smoke & Residue (Field Practical)

Different fires produce different smoke. This affects how hard it is to clean and what methods work.

- **Dry smoke** (high temperature, fast-burning fires): Fine, dry, black or gray residue. Penetrates deeply into porous materials. Often easier to clean than wet smoke but travels farther.
- **Wet smoke** (low temperature, smoldering fires, or fires involving plastics/synthetics): Sticky, smeary, acidic residue. Harder to clean, more odor issues, higher corrosion risk.
- **Protein smoke** (cooking fires, especially with meat or grease): Yellowish, sticky, very strong odor. Penetrates cabinets, HVAC, and fabrics. Often requires aggressive cleaning or replacement of affected contents.
- **Fuel oil / petroleum smoke**: Very heavy, dark, oily residue. Strong odor. Common in furnace or fuel storage fires.
- **Mixed / complex smoke**: Most real jobs. Multiple types depending on what burned.

**Field note**: You can often tell a lot by color, texture, and smell. Black sooty smoke vs yellowish sticky film vs heavy oily coating.

## Common Decision Points

**Structure vs Contents**:
- Heavily burned structural members usually need replacement.
- Smoke-damaged but structurally sound framing can often be cleaned and sealed.
- Contents (furniture, clothing, documents, electronics) are frequently more damaged by smoke and odor than by the fire itself.

**Clean vs Replace**:
- Non-porous surfaces (glass, metal, tile, sealed countertops) can usually be cleaned.
- Porous materials (drywall, insulation, carpet, upholstered furniture, clothing) often cannot be fully restored, especially with heavy or wet smoke.
- Electronics and appliances: High risk of hidden corrosion. Many get replaced even if they "work" initially.

**Odor**:
- Odor is often the biggest long-term complaint.
- Smoke odor can hide in HVAC systems, behind walls, in insulation, and in voids.
- Sealing (encapsulation) + air scrubbing + ozone or hydroxyl treatment are common tools, but results vary.
- Some odors never fully go away without removal of affected materials.

**Post-Fire Water Damage**:
- Firefighters use a lot of water. Many "fire" jobs are actually fire + significant water damage.
- Wet smoke + water = much higher corrosion and mold risk.
- You often end up doing both fire restoration and water restoration techniques on the same job.

## My Working Rules on Fire Jobs

1. **Safety first, always.** Structural and hazmat risks are higher than on water jobs. Slow down on entry.

2. **Document everything heavily.** Photos of burn patterns, smoke residue, and pre-existing conditions protect everyone later.

3. **Assume hidden damage.** Smoke travels through HVAC, walls, attics, and voids. What you see on the surface is rarely the full extent.

4. **Water from firefighters changes everything.** Treat it as a combined fire + water job. Corrosion and mold risk go way up.

5. **Be honest about what can actually be saved.** Over-promising cleanability on heavily smoked porous materials leads to callbacks and unhappy customers.

6. **Odor is emotional.** Even if the structure is clean, lingering smell can make homeowners feel the job isn't done. Address it directly in communication.

## Voice Notes & Prompts

**Voice note**: "On fire jobs, the smoke usually did more damage than the flames. And the water the firefighters used often creates a second water damage problem on top of it."

**Voice usage examples**:
- "Kitchen fire with heavy wet smoke throughout the house, some charring on cabinets — what's the realistic play?"
- "Older home, fire in the attic, lots of smoke damage downstairs — asbestos risk and assessment approach?"
- "Post-fire water damage from suppression, smoke residue on walls and contents — combined approach?"
