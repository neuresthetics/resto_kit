# core/contamination.md — Contamination Control Basics (My Working View)

**Purpose**: Practical contamination control on water and mold jobs. The goal is to protect the clean parts of the house, prevent tracking contamination around, and keep yourself from becoming a vector.

This is especially important on Category 2 and Category 3 jobs, and on any job with visible or suspected mold.

## Core Principle

Once you step into contaminated areas, everything you’re wearing and carrying can spread that contamination. The clean parts of the house should stay clean.

## PPE Discipline (The Biggest Leak)

**Common mistake**: Walking through the clean parts of the house in full PPE after working in contaminated areas.

**My working rules**:
- Take off boot covers, tyvek, and gloves before walking through clean areas whenever possible.
- If you have to move through clean areas in PPE, put down plastic or use designated pathways.
- Change boot covers or wipe boots when moving from dirty to cleaner zones.
- Never sit on furniture or touch clean surfaces while in contaminated PPE.

**Voice note**: "Your suit and boots are dirty the second you step into the loss area. Treat them like they’re covered in sewage — because on Cat 3 jobs, they basically are."

## Containment Basics

Containment is about creating a barrier between the affected area and the rest of the house.

**Simple containment practices**:
- Plastic sheeting on floors and doorways in high-traffic contaminated zones.
- Zip walls or spring-loaded poles for temporary barriers.
- Negative air machines exhausting to the outside when possible (especially on mold or heavy Cat 3 jobs).
- Designated entry/exit points to control where people and equipment go in and out.

**My working rule**: On Category 3 or heavy mold jobs, I set up at least basic containment before doing any significant demo or moving a lot of material through the house.

## Airflow Management

Air moves contamination. Controlling airflow is one of the most effective ways to limit spread.

**Key concepts**:
- **Negative pressure**: Pulling air out of the contaminated area so air flows in from clean areas (instead of contaminated air blowing out).
- **Air scrubbers**: HEPA filtration units that clean the air inside the containment zone.
- **Direction of airflow**: Try to keep air moving from clean → dirty, not the other way around.

**Common mistakes**:
- Running big fans that blow contaminated air into clean parts of the house.
- Not using containment when doing dusty or moldy demo.
- Leaving doors open between affected and unaffected areas.

**My working rule**: On jobs with active mold or heavy contamination, I think about airflow before I start demo. I’d rather set up a negative air machine or air scrubber early than deal with the whole house smelling musty later.

## Foot Traffic & Zoning

**Dirty zone** — The actual loss area (standing water, visible mold, heavy smoke residue).
**Transition zone** — Where you take off or clean PPE before moving to clean areas.
**Clean zone** — Unaffected parts of the house.

**Practical habits**:
- Designate one entry/exit point when possible.
- Keep a box of boot covers and gloves at the transition point.
- Wipe or change boot covers when moving between zones.
- Don’t carry tools or materials from dirty areas into clean areas without cleaning them first.

**My working rule**: I try to minimize how many times I walk back and forth through clean areas while in dirty PPE. Every trip increases the chance of tracking something.

## Tool & Equipment Contamination

Tools and equipment can carry contamination just like PPE.

**Good practices**:
- Keep a set of “dirty area only” tools when possible.
- Wipe down or clean tools before moving them to clean areas.
- Don’t throw contaminated debris into clean trash cans or dumpsters without bagging it.
- Clean or replace vacuum filters and equipment between jobs.

**My working rule**: If a tool has been in standing water or heavy mold, I treat it as contaminated until I clean it properly.

## Common Real-World Leaks

These are things I see techs do that spread contamination:

- Walking through the living room in muddy/wet boots to get something from the truck.
- Taking a smoke break outside then coming back inside still wearing the same dirty gloves and boot covers.
- Using the homeowner’s bathroom while in full PPE.
- Letting debris fall on clean floors while carrying it through the house.
- Running air movers that blow air from the loss area into hallways and bedrooms.

**My working rule**: I try to think like contamination is sticky. Everything that touches the dirty area can carry it somewhere else if I’m not careful.

## Communication with Homeowners

Homeowners notice when you’re careful (or not).

- Explain why you’re setting up containment or changing boot covers.
- Be honest about what areas might be affected.
- Offer to put down extra plastic or use shoe covers in clean areas if it makes them more comfortable.

**Voice note**: "Most homeowners appreciate when you’re careful about not tracking stuff around their house. It shows professionalism."
