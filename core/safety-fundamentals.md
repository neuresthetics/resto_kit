# core/safety-fundamentals.md — Safety Fundamentals for Restoration Work (My Working View)

**Purpose**: Core safety knowledge that every job should be built on. This is the foundation — not the specific red-flag decision logic (that lives in `logic/safety-red-flags.md`).

Safety is non-negotiable. It protects you, your coworkers, the homeowners, and the company. Rushing or cutting corners on safety always costs more in the long run.

## Core Safety Mindset

- **Stop work authority**: You have the right (and responsibility) to stop any job if you feel it’s unsafe. Use it.
- **Assume the worst until proven otherwise**: On fire jobs, older homes, or long-term water losses, assume hidden hazards until you verify otherwise.
- **Complacency kills**: The jobs that feel “routine” are often the ones where people get hurt.
- **Documentation protects everyone**: If something feels off, document it and communicate it.

## Major Hazard Categories on Restoration Jobs

### 1. Electrical Hazards

- Standing water + electricity is one of the fastest ways to get killed or seriously injured.
- Never assume power is off. Verify it.
- All equipment on water or fire jobs must be plugged into GFCI-protected outlets or use GFCI pigtails.
- Burned or damaged electrical panels, outlets, or wiring are common on fire jobs and older water losses.

**My working rule**: If there’s standing water near electrical and I can’t confirm power is off to that area, I stop and get it verified before proceeding.

### 2. Structural Hazards

- Fire-damaged floors, joists, roofs, and stairs can fail without much warning.
- Long-term water damage can rot framing and subfloors.
- Sagging ceilings, bouncy floors, and soft spots underfoot are major red flags.

**My working rule**: On fire jobs and older water losses, I do a deliberate structural walk-through before bringing in heavy equipment or walking deep into compromised areas.

### 3. Biological & Contamination Hazards

- Category 3 water (sewage, flood with organics) carries real health risks.
- Mold exposure can affect people differently — some are very sensitive.
- Long-term water losses often have hidden mold and bacterial growth.

**My working rule**: When in doubt on contamination level, I protect up (higher PPE and containment) rather than down. I can always reduce protection later with more information.

### 4. Chemical & Hazardous Material Hazards

- Older homes can contain asbestos (popcorn ceilings, insulation, flooring, ductwork) and lead paint.
- Some jobs involve unknown chemicals, pesticides, or stored materials in garages and basements.
- Smoke from certain materials (plastics, treated wood, etc.) can be more toxic.

**My working rule**: On pre-1980 homes, especially when disturbing materials, I assume asbestos/lead until proven otherwise or I escalate to someone qualified to handle it.

### 5. Confined Space & Limited Access

- Crawlspaces and attics can have poor air quality, structural issues, and limited escape routes.
- Standing water in crawlspaces increases electrical and slip hazards.

**My working rule**: I assess crawlspaces and attics carefully before entry. Poor lighting, bad air, or structural concerns mean I either improve conditions first or call for help.

### 6. Slips, Trips, Falls & General Site Hazards

- Wet floors, debris, extension cords, and equipment create constant trip hazards.
- Working in occupied homes means being aware of residents, pets, and furniture.

**My working rule**: I keep work areas as clean and organized as reasonably possible. A messy job site is a dangerous job site.

## PPE Basics

- **Minimum on most water jobs**: Gloves, eye protection, and sturdy boots.
- **Elevated protection** (Category 2/3, mold, heavy smoke): Respirator (N95 minimum, often P100 or half-face), tyvek or similar suit, boot covers.
- **Full protection** (heavy Category 3, certain fire jobs, asbestos disturbance): Full PPE including proper respirator, suit, and decontamination procedures.

**My working rule**: PPE is cheap compared to hospital bills or long-term health problems. I’d rather be over-protected than under-protected.

## Working in Occupied Homes

- Many jobs are in homes where people are still living.
- Be aware of children, elderly residents, pets, and people with health issues.
- Communicate clearly about what you’re doing and any hazards you create (cords, wet floors, equipment noise, etc.).

**My working rule**: I treat occupied homes with extra care. The homeowner’s safety and comfort matter.

## Personal Responsibility

- You are responsible for your own safety and the safety of those around you.
- If something doesn’t feel right, speak up.
- Fatigue, rushing, and “just one more thing” thinking cause a lot of injuries.

**My working rule**: At the end of the day, I want to go home in the same condition I arrived. No job is worth getting hurt over.
