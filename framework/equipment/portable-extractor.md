# equipment/portable-extractor.md — Portable Extractors (Practical Field Guide)

**Purpose**: Understand how and when to use portable extractors on water damage jobs. Mechanical water removal is one of the most effective first steps on many jobs.

## Why Extraction Matters

Removing liquid water mechanically is almost always better than trying to dry it in place. The more water you can extract, the less work the air movers and dehumidifiers have to do, and the lower the risk of secondary damage (mold, warping, etc.).

**My working rule**: Extract what you reasonably can before relying on evaporation. Liquid water left behind becomes your enemy on day two and three.

## Types of Portable Extractors

**Small portable extractors** (1–2 motor units):
- Good for smaller jobs, spot extraction, and tight spaces.
- Easier to maneuver in residential settings.
- Lower capacity but very useful for targeted work.

**Larger portable extractors** (dual or triple motor):
- Higher water removal rate.
- Better for bigger residential jobs or when you need serious extraction power.
- Still mobile enough for most houses.

**Truckmounts**:
- Much higher performance.
- Usually used on larger jobs or when maximum extraction is needed.
- Not always practical or available on every job.

**My working rule**: I use the most powerful extractor I have reasonable access to on jobs with significant standing or extractable water. Small portables are great for detail work and smaller losses.

## Wand vs. Tool Extraction

**Wand extraction**:
- Most common method on carpet and pad.
- Good for large open areas.
- Requires good technique to get maximum water removal.

**Tool/stair tool extraction**:
- Useful for stairs, tight areas, under cabinets, and upholstery.
- Allows more targeted extraction in hard-to-reach places.

**My working rule**: I use the right tool for the surface. A wand is great for open carpet, but a stair tool or smaller attachment is often better under cabinets or on stairs.

## Technique Matters

Good extraction technique dramatically improves results:

- Make multiple slow passes.
- Overlap passes.
- Use proper pressure and wand angle.
- Go in different directions when possible.
- Extract until water removal slows significantly.

**My working rule**: I extract until I’m getting very little water on the last few passes. Rushing extraction usually means more work for the drying equipment later.

## When Extraction Is Especially Important

- Class 2 and 3 water losses with carpet and pad.
- Any job with standing water.
- Water under LVP or hardwood (when accessible).
- Category 2 and 3 jobs (removing contaminated water reduces risk).

**My working rule**: The wetter the job, the more aggressive I am with extraction. On heavily saturated jobs, I may make multiple extraction visits in the first 24 hours.

## Limitations

Portable extractors can’t remove water that has already wicked deeply into materials or behind walls/ceilings. They’re best for liquid water that can be reached.

**My working rule**: I use extraction aggressively where I can reach it, then shift to air movement and dehumidification for the remaining moisture.

## Safety & Practical Notes

- Watch for electrical hazards when extracting near outlets or with standing water.
- Use GFCI protection on the extractor.
- Be careful with cords in wet areas.
- Empty recovery tanks regularly to maintain performance.
- Clean and maintain equipment between jobs.
