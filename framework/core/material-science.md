# core/material-science.md — How Materials Actually Behave (My Working View)

**Purpose**: Understand how common building materials respond to water, smoke, heat, and mold. This knowledge feeds directly into decision trees for drying methods, clean vs replace calls, and equipment choices.

This is field-oriented material behavior, not textbook science.

## Drywall / Gypsum Board

**Water behavior**:
- Paper facing wicks water quickly and holds it.
- Gypsum core softens and can crumble when saturated.
- Water wicks upward behind baseboards and into walls easily.
- Once the paper facing delaminates or the core is soft, drying in place is usually a waste of time.

**Smoke / heat behavior**:
- Heavy smoke can penetrate the paper facing and leave odor.
- Charred or heavily smoked drywall is almost always replaced.

**Mold behavior**:
- Very mold-friendly once wet. The paper facing is food.
- Hidden mold behind drywall is extremely common on jobs older than 48–72 hours.

**My working rule**: If the drywall is soft, has visible mold, or has been wet more than 48 hours in warm conditions, I usually cut it out rather than try to dry it.

## Wood (Framing, Subfloor, Hardwood)

**Water behavior**:
- Absorbs water and swells. Can cup, warp, or check as it dries.
- Dimensional lumber (framing) is more forgiving than finished hardwood.
- Subfloor can wick water long distances under finished flooring.

**Smoke / heat behavior**:
- Can absorb smoke odor deeply, especially softwoods.
- Charred structural wood usually needs replacement or heavy sanding + sealing.

**Mold behavior**:
- Mold grows on the surface and can penetrate into checks and end grain.

**My working rule**: Hardwood floors with significant cupping or long-term saturation are often replaced. Framing can usually be dried and treated if caught early.

## Concrete / Slab

**Water behavior**:
- Absorbs water but releases it slowly.
- Can hold moisture for a long time under flooring or in pores.
- Moisture can wick up through the slab from below (especially in older homes or high water table areas).

**Smoke / heat behavior**:
- Usually unaffected structurally by smoke, but can absorb odor in porous areas.

**Mold behavior**:
- Mold doesn’t grow well on clean concrete itself, but grows easily on dust, adhesives, or materials on top of it.

**My working rule**: Concrete usually dries eventually, but it can hold moisture under flooring and cause secondary issues. Good airflow and dehumidification are critical.

## Insulation

**Fiberglass batts**:
- Holds water like a sponge. Once wet, it loses R-value and is very hard to dry properly.
- Mold grows easily on the paper or dust on the surface.

**Cellulose**:
- Extremely absorbent. Holds a lot of water and is very prone to mold and odor retention.

**Spray foam (closed cell)**:
- Much more resistant to water. Usually can be dried if the water wasn’t contaminated.

**My working rule**: Wet fiberglass or cellulose insulation is almost always removed. Closed-cell spray foam has a much better chance of being saved.

## Carpet & Pad

**Water behavior**:
- Carpet itself can often be dried if extracted quickly.
- Pad is the problem — it holds water, wicks it, and grows mold fast.
- Water under LVP or hardwood from wet carpet/pad is very common.

**Smoke behavior**:
- Holds smoke odor extremely well, especially protein smoke or wet smoke.

**My working rule**: On anything more than light water or smoke, I usually recommend removing the pad. Carpet can sometimes be cleaned and dried, but pad is rarely worth saving once truly wet.

## LVP (Luxury Vinyl Plank) & Sheet Vinyl

**Water behavior**:
- The material itself doesn’t absorb much, but water gets underneath easily and wicks under adjacent planks.
- Creates a vapor barrier that makes drying from above very difficult.

**Smoke behavior**:
- Can trap smoke residue and odor underneath.

**My working rule**: Lifting edges or creating gaps is often necessary to dry underneath. On heavy contamination, removal is frequently the better option.

## Cabinetry & Particle Board / MDF

**Water behavior**:
- Particle board and MDF swell and disintegrate when wet. Almost never worth trying to save once swollen.
- Solid wood cabinets can sometimes be dried and refinished, but particle board cores usually cannot.

**Smoke behavior**:
- Holds odor very well, especially inside drawers and behind doors.

**My working rule**: If the cabinets are particle board/MDF and got wet, plan on replacement. Solid wood has a better chance.

## Electronics & Appliances

**Water + smoke behavior**:
- Very high risk of hidden corrosion, especially when acidic smoke combines with moisture.
- Can work initially and then fail weeks or months later.

**My working rule**: Most electronics and major appliances on fire or heavy smoke jobs get replaced. The risk of future failure is too high to justify cleaning in most cases.

## Fabrics, Clothing & Upholstery

**Water behavior**:
- Can usually be cleaned and dried if addressed quickly.
- Heavy soiling or long-term water often leads to replacement.

**Smoke behavior**:
- Protein smoke and wet smoke are very difficult to remove from fabrics.
- Odor can be nearly impossible to eliminate on some items.

**My working rule**: On heavy smoke jobs, especially protein or wet smoke, I’m often honest with homeowners that many soft goods may need to be replaced even after professional cleaning.

## Paper, Documents & Books

**Water behavior**:
- Inks can run. Pages stick together as they dry.
- Freeze drying or professional document restoration is often worth it for important items.

**Smoke behavior**:
- Odor penetrates deeply and is hard to remove.

**My working rule**: Important documents and photos should be handled by specialists when possible. Normal drying often causes more damage.
