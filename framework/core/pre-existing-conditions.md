# core/pre-existing-conditions.md — Identifying & Documenting Pre-Existing Damage (My Working View)

**Purpose**: Learn how to spot, document, and handle pre-existing conditions on restoration jobs. This protects you, the company, and the claim from disputes later.

Pre-existing conditions are damage or issues that existed **before** the current loss event. Failing to identify and document them is one of the fastest ways to create problems with homeowners and insurance companies.

## Why This Matters

- Homeowners sometimes try to get old damage fixed under the current claim.
- Insurance companies want clear documentation of what is new vs old.
- Missing pre-existing damage can lead to scope disputes, denied supplements, or accusations of poor workmanship.
- Good documentation builds credibility and protects everyone.

## Common Types of Pre-Existing Conditions

**Water-related**:
- Old water stains on ceilings or walls (often yellowish/brown and dry)
- Previous water damage that was poorly repaired or never fully dried
- Efflorescence or mineral deposits on concrete (sign of long-term moisture)
- Rot or soft spots in framing/subfloor from old leaks
- Musty smells that were already present

**Smoke/Fire-related**:
- Old smoke odor or residue from previous fires or heavy smoking
- Previously charred or heat-damaged materials that were never replaced
- Old smoke stains on walls or ceilings

**General wear & tear**:
- Worn, stained, or damaged flooring (especially carpet and LVP)
- Scratches, dents, and dings on walls, doors, and trim
- Outdated or failing finishes (old paint, worn countertops, etc.)
- Previous repairs that were done poorly

**Mold-related**:
- Old mold stains or growth that was painted over or partially cleaned
- Musty odor that existed before the current water event

**Other**:
- Previous structural issues or settling cracks
- Prior pest damage
- Old asbestos or lead-based materials

## How to Identify Pre-Existing Conditions in the Field

**Visual clues**:
- Stains that look old and dry (vs fresh wet stains)
- Inconsistent damage patterns (e.g., water stains in areas unrelated to the current loss)
- Materials that don’t match surrounding areas (different drywall texture, paint color, flooring, etc.)
- Signs of previous repairs (patching, painting, new sections of flooring or trim)

**Smell clues**:
- Musty odor that doesn’t match the current water event timeline
- Old smoke smell unrelated to the current fire

**Homeowner clues**:
- They mention “it’s been like that for years” or “we had a leak there before”
- They try to point out unrelated damage during your assessment

**My working rule**: If something looks old, inconsistent with the current loss, or the homeowner mentions it casually, I treat it as potentially pre-existing until I document it properly.

## Documentation Best Practices

**Photograph everything**:
- Take clear photos of any suspected pre-existing damage **before** you start work.
- Include wide shots that show context + close-ups of the damage.
- Note the location (room, wall, ceiling, floor, etc.).

**Written notes**:
- Describe what you see and why you believe it’s pre-existing.
- Record any statements the homeowner makes about prior damage.
- Note the condition of surrounding materials for comparison.

**Communication**:
- Point out pre-existing damage to the homeowner early and document their response.
- If working with an adjuster or property manager, flag pre-existing items clearly in your scope and photos.

**My working rule**: I over-document rather than under-document. A few extra photos of old damage have saved me from many headaches later.

## How Pre-Existing Conditions Affect Decisions

- You are generally **not responsible** for fixing pre-existing damage unless it was made worse by your work or the current loss event.
- Clearly separate new damage from old damage in your scope and estimate.
- Sometimes pre-existing damage makes the current job more difficult or expensive (e.g., old rot that now needs to be addressed).

**My working rule**: I clearly communicate what is new vs pre-existing to everyone involved. I don’t want to be blamed later for damage that was already there.

## Common Pitfalls

- Assuming all damage is from the current event.
- Not taking photos of questionable areas before starting work.
- Letting homeowners pressure you into including old damage in the current scope without documentation.
- Ignoring pre-existing conditions because “it’s not that bad” — it can still become a dispute later.

**My working rule**: If I’m unsure whether something is pre-existing, I document it thoroughly and note it in my report. It’s better to flag it than to get surprised later.
