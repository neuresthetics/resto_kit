# core/corrosion-basics.md — Corrosion Basics on Restoration Jobs (My Working View)

**Purpose**: Understand how corrosion happens on fire, smoke, and water jobs — especially when smoke and moisture are combined. This is critical for electronics, appliances, and metal components.

Corrosion is often invisible at first but can cause major problems weeks or months after the job is "done."

## Why Corrosion Happens on These Jobs

Corrosion is the deterioration of metal caused by chemical reactions, usually involving oxygen and moisture. On restoration jobs, it is accelerated by:

- **Acidic smoke residue** (especially from burning plastics, treated materials, or certain contents)
- **Moisture** (from the original water loss or from firefighters)
- **Time** (corrosion can continue developing long after the visible water and smoke are gone)
- **Heat** (from the fire itself)

**Fire + Water = High Corrosion Risk**

When a fire is extinguished with water, you often get acidic smoke residue + moisture. This combination is particularly aggressive on metals and electronics.

**My working rule**: On any fire job that also had significant water (from the fire or suppression), I assume higher corrosion risk on metals and electronics.

## Materials Most Affected by Corrosion

**Electronics & Appliances**:
- Very high risk. Smoke + moisture can cause corrosion on circuit boards, connections, and components.
- Items may work initially and then fail later.
- Common on TVs, computers, appliances, security systems, etc.

**Metal Components**:
- Ductwork, HVAC coils, metal framing, pipes, electrical boxes, hardware.
- Can corrode over time, especially in hidden areas.

**Structural Metals**:
- Less common but possible on severe fires with heavy water use.
- Usually more resistant than electronics.

**My working rule**: I treat electronics and appliances on fire + water jobs as high-risk for future failure. I’m often honest that replacement is the safer recommendation.

## Factors That Increase Corrosion Risk

- Acidic smoke (plastics, synthetic materials, certain contents)
- Moisture left in contact with metals
- High humidity environments after the job
- Time (corrosion can continue developing for weeks/months)
- Poor ventilation

**My working rule**: The longer moisture and smoke residue stay in contact with metals, the higher the corrosion risk. Quick extraction and drying help, but hidden areas are still vulnerable.

## Practical Field Implications

**Electronics & Appliances**:
- High chance of hidden corrosion even if they appear to work.
- Cleaning is difficult and often not reliable long-term.
- Most techs and companies recommend replacement on anything more than very light smoke exposure when moisture was also present.

**HVAC Systems**:
- Coils and metal ductwork can corrode.
- Smoke residue inside the system can continue causing issues.

**Structural / Hidden Areas**:
- Metal in walls, ceilings, or crawlspaces can be affected if smoke and moisture reached them.

**My working rule**: On fire jobs with water involvement, I flag electronics, appliances, and HVAC components early for discussion with the adjuster/homeowner about potential replacement.

## What Can Be Done

- **Source removal**: Remove heavily contaminated materials.
- **Cleaning**: Possible on some metal surfaces, but difficult on electronics and complex components.
- **Drying**: Fast drying reduces corrosion risk but doesn’t eliminate it if acidic residue remains.
- **Sealing / coatings**: Sometimes used on structural metals.
- **Replacement**: Often the most reliable option for electronics and appliances.

**My working rule**: I don’t over-promise on saving electronics or appliances after significant smoke + moisture exposure. The risk of future failure is real.

## Communication Points

- Be upfront with homeowners and adjusters about corrosion risk on electronics and appliances.
- Document the presence of smoke + moisture on metal components.
- Recommend professional evaluation when the value or importance of items is high.

**My working rule**: It’s better to have the conversation about potential replacement early than to have items fail after the job is closed.
